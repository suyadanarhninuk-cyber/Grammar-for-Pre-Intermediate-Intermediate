Present Perfect or Past Simple?

Choose the correct tense for each sentence.

Key rule: Use the present perfect when there is a connection to NOW (unfinished time, no specific past time, result matters now). Use the past simple when the time is finished or stated (last week, in 2005, yesterday).
Question 1
I ______ a rough plan this morning. (it is still morning)
"This morning" — the time period is not finished yet (it's still morning), so we use the present perfect. If it were the afternoon, we'd say "I wrote a plan this morning" (past simple — the morning is finished).
Question 2
I ______ a lot of time last week.
"Last week" is a specific, finished time in the past. When we state when something happened, we must use the past simple. You cannot use the present perfect with last week, yesterday, in 2010, etc.
Question 3
______ to Buenos Aires? (at any point in your life)
We're asking about any point before now — the time is not specific. "Ever" is a classic present perfect time expression used in questions. The experience is connected to the present (have you experienced this at any time in your life up to now?).
Question 4
I ______ plenty of information. (and I will use it to write my essay now)
The action happened at an unstated time in the past, and the result is connected to the present (I have the information now and will use it). This is a core use of the present perfect — past action, present relevance.
Question 5
She ______ really hard for the last two weeks.
"For the last two weeks" — the situation started in the past and continues to now. We use the present perfect (simple or continuous) with for (length of time) and since (point in time) to show this connection. Both "she's worked" and "she's been working" would be acceptable here.

Present Perfect Simple or Continuous?

Choose the best form based on what the speaker wants to emphasise.

Key difference:
• Present perfect continuous → emphasises HOW LONG or the activity itself (may be unfinished)
• Present perfect simple → emphasises the RESULT or HOW MANY TIMES (completed)
Question 6
I ______ my essay. Here it is! (focus: it is now finished — the result)
The focus is on the result — the essay is now finished and ready. The present perfect simple emphasises completion. "I've been writing my essay" would mean the focus is on the activity — and we wouldn't know if it's finished or not.
Question 7
I ______ my essay. That's why I'm so tired! (focus: the activity itself, how long)
The focus is on the activity itself and its duration — it explains why the speaker is tired. The present perfect continuous emphasises the ongoing process. We don't know (or care) if the essay is finished — what matters is the effect of the long activity.
Question 8
I ______ three articles this week. (focus: how many — the number)
The focus is on how many (three articles) — this is about completed quantity. The present perfect simple is used when we count or quantify completed actions. The continuous form focuses on duration, not counting.
Question 9
I ______ them since I was a child. (verb: know)
"Know" is a state verb. State verbs do NOT generally have a continuous form — not even in the present perfect. We say "I've known them" (NOT "I've been knowing them"). Other state verbs: believe, want, like, love, hate, own, belong, understand.
Question 10
It's the longest essay I ______ to write.
With expressions like "It's the first / best / longest / only time…" we always use the present perfect. These structures connect a superlative or ordinal to the speaker's experience up to now. "Ever" reinforces that we mean "at any point in your life."
