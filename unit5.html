1

Which Future Form?

Choose the correct future form based on the context.

Quick Guide:
• Present Continuous → definite arrangements already made (booked, organised, confirmed)
• Going to → plans/intentions already decided (but maybe not arranged yet) + predictions from present evidence
• Will → predictions based on opinion/experience + spontaneous decisions + offers/promises + facts about the future
Question 1
We ______ in a small hotel near the beach. We booked it last week. (a definite arrangement already made)
Present continuous — the hotel is already booked, so this is a definite arrangement. We use the present continuous for future plans where specific arrangements have been made (time, place, bookings confirmed).
Question 2
I think it ______ extremely hot there. (a prediction based on what you think/believe)
Will — "I think" signals a prediction based on opinion or past experience, not on present evidence. We use will for predictions that come from what we think, believe, or expect. (Going to would be used if we could SEE evidence right now.)
Question 3
We ______ a bus to get around the island. That's our plan. (a plan/intention already decided but not yet arranged)
Going to — they've already thought about it and decided ("that's our plan"), but they haven't made the actual arrangements yet (no booking). Going to expresses intentions and plans that have been decided but may not be fully arranged.
Question 4
A: "We need someone to collect the tickets." B: "Don't worry, I ______ them." (a spontaneous decision — decided at the moment of speaking)
Will — this is a spontaneous decision made at the moment of speaking. The speaker hadn't planned this before — they just decided now. We also use will for offers and promises: "I'll help you", "I'll let you know."
Question 5
Look at those dark clouds! It ______. (a prediction based on present evidence you can see)
Going to — there is present evidence (dark clouds) that leads to this prediction. When we can see, hear, or feel something NOW that tells us what will happen, we use going to. Compare: "I think it'll rain tomorrow" (opinion, no evidence → will).
Question 6
The manager ______ a party just after we get back. (a scheduled/organised event with a future time expression)
Present continuous — this is a definite arrangement that has been organised. The time expression ("just after we get back") and the fact that it's a party (which requires planning) tell us arrangements have been made.
2

Will vs Going to — Key Distinctions

Focus on the difference between will and going to.

Question 7
The best player on the tour ______ a special trophy. (a future fact — not personal, not a plan)
Will — this is a future fact or event that is not personal. We use will to talk about future events that are general facts, official arrangements, or scheduled events that are not about personal plans. "The prime minister will open the debate tomorrow" follows the same pattern.
Question 8
We've decided — we ______ a boat to a couple of the islands. (a decision already made before now)
Going to — "We've decided" tells us the decision was already made before now. Going to is for plans and intentions that have been thought about and decided. Will would suggest a spontaneous decision made right now.
Question 9
Tell me all about it and I ______ the information to the rest of the team. (a promise made right now)
Will — this is a promise being made at the moment of speaking. We use will for offers, promises, and spontaneous decisions: "I'll help you", "I'll let everyone know", "Don't worry, I'll do it."
Question 10
Well, looking at all our plans, we ______ a very varied trip! (a prediction based on what you can see/know — present evidence from the plans)
Going to — the speaker is looking at the plans (present evidence) and making a prediction based on what they can see. When we have evidence in front of us for a prediction, we use going to, not will.
Question 11
We ______ probably stay in some sort of mountain lodge there. (a prediction about future events not yet arranged — opinion)
Will — "probably" signals this is a prediction based on what the speaker thinks, not a definite plan. Nothing has been decided or arranged — the speaker is speculating. Will is used for predictions based on opinion, belief, or expectation.
Question 12
Your motives ______ you choose the best course for your aims and goals. (a general future fact — impersonal, always true)
Will — this is a general future fact from an information guide — it's impersonal and true for everyone, not a personal plan or intention. We use will for future facts, general truths about the future, and impersonal statements.
