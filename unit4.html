Past Perfect or Past Simple?

Choose the correct tense for each sentence.

Key rule: Use the past perfect (had + past participle) to show that one event happened BEFORE another event in the past. Use the past simple when events are in chronological order or there is only one past time frame.
Question 1
When I got home, my husband ______ dinner. (= he cooked it BEFORE I arrived — it was ready)
The cooking happened BEFORE the speaker arrived home. We use the past perfect to show the earlier event. Compare: "When I got home, my husband cooked dinner" = I arrived, THEN he cooked (chronological order → both past simple).
Question 2
His grandfather ______ a musician and his father was also a composer.
The grandfather's career as a musician happened earlier than the father's career as a composer. The past perfect shows the grandfather came first in time. The past simple ("his father was also a composer") is the later reference point.
Question 3
They travelled to Vienna and ______ sensational reports of Mozart's talent.
The events are in chronological order: first they travelled, then they heard. When events are listed in the order they happened, we use the past simple for both. The past perfect is only needed when the order is reversed.
Question 4
By the time he was 17, Mozart's reputation ______ to spread through Europe.
"By the time" is a classic past perfect trigger. The reputation spreading started before he turned 17. "Already" goes between the auxiliary (had) and the main verb (begun): had already begun.
Question 5
When Mozart was born, five of his siblings ______ in infancy.
The siblings died before Mozart was born. "When" + past simple gives the reference point, and the past perfect shows what happened even earlier. This is the classic pattern: when + past simple, past perfect.
Question 6
His family were richer than they ______ before.
We're comparing their wealth at one point in the past with any time before that point. The past perfect with "ever" and "before" shows the earlier reference. This is like the present perfect's "have you ever…" but shifted back to the past.

Past Perfect Simple or Continuous?

Choose the best form based on what the speaker wants to emphasise.

Key difference:
• Past perfect continuous (had been + -ing) → emphasises HOW LONG or the activity itself leading up to a past moment
• Past perfect simple (had + pp) → emphasises the RESULT or COMPLETION before a past moment
• State verbs → always use the simple form (had known, NOT had been knowing)
Question 7
Times were hard and the family ______ for some time. (focus: how long the struggle lasted)
The focus is on how long the activity continued ("for some time"). The past perfect continuous emphasises the duration and ongoing nature of the struggle leading up to that point in the past.
Question 8
By the time he was six, the little boy ______ a composition of his own. (focus: completed result)
The focus is on the completed result — the composition was finished. The past perfect simple emphasises that the action was complete before the past reference point (age six). "Had been writing" would suggest the process was still ongoing.
Question 9
I knew the way because I ______ her several times before.
"Several times" counts the number of visits — this is about how many, not how long. We cannot use the past perfect continuous to say how many times something happened. Use the past perfect simple for counting completed actions.
Question 10
The politician ______ to be re-elected, but in the end she only got ten per cent of the vote.
This describes an unfulfilled hope — something that was expected but didn't happen. We use the past perfect simple with verbs like expected, hoped, wanted, planned, intended to express past disappointments. Note: "had been hoping" is also possible here for emphasis on the ongoing nature of the hope, but "had expected" is the standard form for unfulfilled expectations.
